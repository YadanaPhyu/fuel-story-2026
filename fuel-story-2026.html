Where does Middle East oil actually go — and who breaks if it stops?

The Strait of Hormuz is a 33km waterway. About 20 million barrels of oil pass through it every single day. Right now, in March 2026, it is effectively shut. Here is what the numbers say about who suffers most — and why the answer is almost entirely Asia.

March 26, 2026 · 10 min read · Sparked by a BBC news segment
Live context: The US-Iran conflict that began late February 2026 has led to a blockade of the Strait of Hormuz. Brent crude has crossed $104 per barrel. Bangladesh has begun rationing fuel. Asian refineries are invoking force majeure. Myanmar has enforced odd-even driving rules. This story is happening now.
Zero The global picture first

Before we get to who is most exposed, let's look at the full map. The Middle East produces about 32% of the world's oil. But production is only half the story — what matters geopolitically is who depends on that production to keep their economies running.

Europe gets about 12% of its crude from the Gulf — significant, but manageable because European nations hold mandatory 90-day emergency reserves and have diversified suppliers including Norway, the US, and until recently, Russia. The Americas barely register. That leaves Asia absorbing nearly three-quarters of everything the Middle East ships out.

The asymmetry that defines this crisis
The Middle East sells 72% of its oil to Asia. But when the Strait shuts, the US feels almost nothing. Europe feels a price shock. Asia loses the fuel that runs its factories, farms, and commutes. Same crisis. Completely different exposure.
One Why Asia? The geopolitics behind the numbers

This lopsided dependency didn't happen by accident. It is the product of decades of deliberate policy choices, one energy revolution, and the simple geography of where Asia's oil fields ran dry.

1973
The oil shock that changed everything for Asia
OPEC's oil embargo hits Western nations hardest, but Japan — importing 99% of its oil — nearly collapses economically. It forces Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan to build strategic reserves and lock in long-term Gulf supply deals. The dependency is institutionalized.
1980s
The US becomes the Gulf's security guarantor
America stations naval forces in the Gulf, signs security agreements with Saudi Arabia, and essentially guarantees the free flow of oil — in exchange for Gulf states pricing oil in US dollars. Asia benefits from this security umbrella without paying for it.
1993
China crosses the line — from exporter to importer
China's domestic oil consumption exceeds production for the first time. It begins sourcing from the Middle East aggressively, locking in supply deals with Saudi Arabia, Iran, and Iraq. By the 2010s, China becomes the world's largest crude importer.
2008–2015
The US shale revolution — and its quiet geopolitical consequence
Hydraulic fracking unlocks vast domestic oil reserves. US production doubles. By 2020, the US becomes a net oil exporter for the first time since 1949. Washington begins stepping back from Middle East security commitments. The risk doesn't disappear — it transfers to Asia.
2020s
Asia industrialises faster than it can diversify
China, India, Vietnam, Bangladesh, and the Philippines drive the world's fastest economic growth — all powered by oil they import from one region. Renewables are growing but cannot displace baseload demand fast enough. The dependency deepens every year.
2026
The bill comes due
The US-Iran war shuts the Strait of Hormuz. The US, which receives just 2.5% of Hormuz oil, feels a price bump. Asia, which receives 89%, faces an existential supply crisis. Decades of dependency, compressed into weeks.
The shale revolution's hidden consequence
When the US stopped needing Middle East oil, it also stopped feeling urgency to protect the region. The security umbrella quietly shrank. Asia kept importing. The risk didn't disappear — it just stopped being America's problem.
Two Where does the oil go? The Hormuz breakdown

The Strait of Hormuz is where the geopolitics becomes a number. About 20 million barrels pass through its 33km width every single day — roughly 25% of all global seaborne oil trade. Here is exactly who receives it.

The number that matters
89% of all Hormuz oil goes to Asia. China alone takes 38%. The US takes 2.5%. This is not a shared global crisis. It is an Asian crisis that the rest of the world watches through a price ticker.
Three Who exports through it?

Six Gulf states — Saudi Arabia, Iraq, UAE, Kuwait, Iran, and Qatar — depend on this single passage to reach the world market. Before the blockade, Saudi Arabia was routing nearly 90% of its oil exports through the strait. There is almost no meaningful bypass.

Why there is no plan B
Saudi Arabia and the UAE have combined pipeline bypass capacity of about 2.6 million barrels per day — a fraction of their own daily production. Most Gulf states have no alternative route at all. The Strait of Hormuz is not a bottleneck. It is the only road.
Four Who breaks first?

Strategic petroleum reserves tell you how long a country can survive without new imports. The lower the number, the faster the crisis moves from global headline to local petrol station. The globally recommended minimum is 90 days.

Critical — under 30 days Vulnerable — 30–60 days Exposed — 60–100 days Buffered — 100+ days
Myanmar — a closer look

40 days. 3% domestic production. One-third of all imports is fuel.

Myanmar's domestic oil production covers only 3–5% of total fuel demand. Everything else is imported. With just 40 days of strategic reserves — well below the recommended 90 — the country has almost no buffer against a prolonged supply shock. Fuel accounts for roughly one-third of all Myanmar's imports, meaning a price spike doesn't just hit the pump. It strains foreign exchange reserves, pushes inflation across food and transport, and tightens the government's ability to import anything else. The military government has already enforced an odd-even driving rule in response to the current crisis. On March 5, the Central Bank released $32 million to oil traders just to stabilize supply. These are emergency measures — and emergency measures have a time limit.

Five ASEAN is on the front line

Within Southeast Asia, the dependence on Persian Gulf crude is acute. Most ASEAN nations have spent decades industrializing on the assumption that Gulf oil would always be available, affordable, and flowing. That assumption is now being stress-tested.

The Philippines in this story
95% of Philippine crude imports come from the Persian Gulf. The country has 50–60 days of reserves. Filipino jeepney drivers are already feeling this — fuel prices are rising while the government scrambles for alternatives.
Six The bigger picture
"The shale revolution gave America energy independence. What it quietly did at the same time was transfer 50 years of geopolitical risk onto Asia — and Asia had no shale revolution of its own."

Every major oil shock in history has generated a structural policy response. The 1973 embargo accelerated France's nuclear programme. The 1979 Iranian Revolution drove Japan's aggressive energy-efficiency push and reserve-building. The current disruption arrives in a more complicated moment: Asia is electrifying faster than ever, but the transition is incomplete. Coal, oil, and gas still run the region's factories, farms, and transport networks.

For Myanmar, the Philippines, Vietnam, and Bangladesh — countries without deep reserves, without diversified suppliers, and without the fiscal space to absorb price shocks — the urgency is not abstract. It is already at the petrol station, at the fertilizer supplier, at the farm gate. A fuel shortage is not only an energy problem. It is an inflation problem, a food security problem, and a political stability problem, all arriving at once.

The countries that emerge from this crisis in a stronger position will be the ones that treat it as a forcing function — not a temporary disruption to wait out, but a structural signal that the old energy geography no longer holds.
